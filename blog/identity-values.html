A situation where you had an ethical decision to make:

Back in university, we had a project-based paper with a team member who was engaged and dedicated, but very difficult to work with. As the project continued further on, she was becoming increasingly agitated and withdrawn, and had tendencies to voice frustrations on the other group members and our tutor, which was uncomfortable for us and had a bad effect on the group dynamic.
After a few instances of this and talking amongst ourselves, we decided to try and find out if there was a different way she would prefer to work that suited her better, to hopefully ease tensions and improve the situation. At the start she was fairly dismissive and not willing to discuss anything.
But, as time went on we slowly started to find out she had a really high workload (other demanding papers, work outside of uni, heavily involved in associations taking up her time, financial concerns etc) and was stretched thin and was feeling generally overwhelmed. To ease some of the pressure, we looked for ways to reduce some of the implied workload from our own project by being mindful to keep meetings short and concise and reduce some of her expected tasks. On top of this, we were careful to keep the mood relaxed and easy.
While avoiding conflict and without doing anything too drastic, the result was pretty effective. The overall group dynamic improved again and efforts were focused more constructively.
Although we never formally addressed it, I think everyone came to an understanding and it helped us have a healthy project outcome as improving the relationship throughout the team.

Using the evidence gathered, describe your strengths and limitations

My family has always had an appreciation for travel, as well as curiosity and openness to the world around us and I think that has imparted on my own values. I am very fortunate to have been able to travel from a young age and see how other cultures live.
This was a bit of a double edged sword however, as I didn’t really want to settle down after finishing high-school, and I ended up dropping out in my final year, saving up some money and moving to Australia. When I did finally come back however, I felt more ready and motivated and had a strong sense of direction, which has been highly beneficial in moving towards my goals going forward.

Identify which of your strengths might help you in your learning journey and how they might intersect with learning obstacles

I think having some prior life experience is beneficial and an asset, both in terms of personal development, as well as keeping perspective during the course. Over the years I have had a chance to get to know my own style of learning, workflow and identify strategies that work for me.

Share an example from your experience of where you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension.

One example that happened pretty recently was actually over this summer break. My girlfriend and I went on a camping trip together down in the South Island and kept finding ourselves arguing and running into conflict about setting up our camp and packing up again.
Although it sounds silly writing it out now, we would clash on this and annoy each other.
She is someone who values order and keeping things tidy, while I am more free-flowing and pragmatic. We live together at home, and manage to keep an equilibrium there, but somehow this new unfamiliar setting and slightly cramped environment threw our system out of balance.
After taking our time to grump, (and get some breakfast and coffee in, which always helps), we talked about how each of us wanted things done and worked out a solution together to respect each other's way of working.
We found a much better flow with this and didn’t argue about it again, and the vibes improved considerably as a result. We laughed about the whole thing after, because it seems pretty silly now.

Lawrence Goodhead 2022
